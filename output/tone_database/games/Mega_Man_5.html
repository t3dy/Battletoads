Sound Driver: Capcom

Capcom's internal NES sound engine, evolved across the Mega Man series (MM1-MM6) and Disney licensed titles. Known for extremely dense drum programming and bright pulse tones.

Octave + semitone byte. Full note identity in one byte — no separate octave command needed. Variable-width command dispatch.

Sonic signature: Bright, driving, rhythmic. Heavy noise channel usage (often 700+ hits per 80 seconds). Dual-duty pulse arrangement: 50% lead, 25% harmony. The most 'band-like' sound on NES.

How the NES hardware makes this sound

How the NES hardware makes this sound

Fixed triangle waveform — no duty cycle control, no volume control (only on/off gating via the linear counter). Produces a frequency one octave LOWER than pulse channels for the same period value (32-step vs 16-step sequencer).

How to recreate in REAPER (ReapNES Console)

In ReapNES Console: Slider 33 = Channel Mode (set to 2 for Triangle). CC11 is always 127 (gate signal only). No CC12 (no duty cycle). Volume is either full or silent — articulation comes from note duration only.

What it sounds like

Pure triangle wave bass. No timbre variation possible — all expression comes from note timing. Consistent volume throughout the note with minimal decay. Creates a smooth, legato feel. Common in RPG town themes and slower pieces. ADSR: zero attack, minimal decay, high sustain. Mixed bass articulation. 275 notes captured.

Role in the mix

Bass. The only channel that can produce sub-bass frequencies. Staccato triangle = punchy bass hits. Legato triangle = sustained bass lines. The lack of volume control makes it the 'loudest' channel in the mix.

How the NES hardware makes this sound

Linear-feedback shift register (LFSR) with 16 selectable periods and 2 modes (long = white noise, short = metallic/tonal). 4-bit volume with hardware envelope. No pitch in the traditional sense — period controls 'brightness'.

How to recreate in REAPER (ReapNES Console)

In ReapNES Console: Slider 33 = Channel Mode (set to 3 for Noise). Note number maps to drum sound (36=kick, 38=snare, 42=hi-hat). Velocity sets initial volume. ADSR envelope shapes the hit.

What it sounds like

Percussion channel. 720 hits captured. Dense drum pattern — continuous beats. NES noise uses a shift register, not samples — every 'drum' is synthesized from filtered noise.

Role in the mix

Percussion. Low periods = hi-hats and cymbals. Mid periods = snare. High periods = kick drums. Some games use noise for wind/explosion effects. The short LFSR mode creates metallic tones used for distinctive 'chiptune snares'.
